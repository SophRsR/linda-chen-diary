# "I Found My Daughter's Diary, But She No Longer Exists"

**Please visit [Jane Doe's Diary](https://SophRsR.github.io/linda-chen-diary/) to see the whole project**

---


So... It all started with the topic of my Writing 50 assignment — Medical Horror 👻


I originally wrote the story as part of my Chinese composition class, but my teacher at that time felt the story was too dark and she couldn't understand why someone would be "dumb enough" to replace every organ in their body. But this time I decided to give it another shot — after all, being WEIRD is the spirit of a horror story!

Hope you enjoy it :)

## The story

Jane gets an eyelid surgery when she's 18. A minor procedure. Her mom signs the consent form. It's supposed to be one thing, just this once — a small improvement, and then she'll stop.

She doesn't stop.

Each surgery makes her notice something else she wants to fix. One procedure justifies the next. By 22, she has replaced every organ in her body. Her hands. Her skin. Her brain. Her heart. Zero percent of her original DNA remains. She is, quite literally, no longer herself.

The story comes to us through Jane's diary — twelve entries she left behind — and through a Twitter thread written by her mother, Linda, who is posting after the worst has already happened. Linda isn't trying to go viral. She's a desperate parent who watched her daughter disappear and is now saying, publicly, *please don't let this happen to yours*.

## Why a diary? Why Twitter?

I chose the diary because I needed you to see Jane from the inside. Not as a cautionary statistic. Not as a headline. As a girl who decorated pages with stickers and doodles, who took selfies with her friends on her 18th birthday, who wrote things like "I just want to feel pretty" — and meant it.

But I also needed you to see Jane from the outside, the way her mother saw her. That's what the Twitter frame does. Linda is posting her daughter's diary pages to the same platform where teenagers scroll through beauty content every day. The same platform that shapes the standards Jane was chasing. The frame is part of the point.

## The details that do the work

The visual argument of the diary matters as much as the text. I didn't want to *tell* you Jane was losing herself. I wanted the pages to make you feel it before you noticed it.

**The handwriting.** In the early entries, it's loose and cursive — a teenager's handwriting. By the end, it's stiff, uniform, almost printed. The self leaving the page.

**The color.** The first pages are warm and saturated. Gradually, entry by entry, the color drains. The last pages are nearly grey. The change is so slow you don't register it happening — which is exactly how Jane lost herself, one procedure at a time.

**The stickers and drawings.** Early entries are full of them: little decorations, doodles in the margins, personality spilling everywhere. They thin out. Then they're gone. A person becoming a template.

**The birthday photos.** At 18, Jane is surrounded by friends who all look different from each other — different faces, different backgrounds, different people. At 28, she's surrounded by friends who all look identical to her. Same face, copied and pasted. A group photo where individuality has been surgically removed.

**The AI entries.** The last three diary entries were rewritten by me using AI — intentionally. Cosmetic surgery standardizes faces. AI standardizes voice. It's the same impulse: hand yourself over to something that promises to make you better, smoother, more acceptable — and lose the things that made your writing (or your face) yours in the first place.

## The replies

The four comments on Linda's thread aren't filler. Each one models a different real-world voice in this conversation:

- A surgeon urging professional responsibility
- A young woman who sees herself in Jane's story
- A journalist framing the systemic issue
- A parent who almost said yes

I wanted the thread to feel alive — like a conversation you might actually stumble into while scrolling, complete with likes and retweet counts that make you feel you're witnessing something already in motion.

## A note on the platform

There's a loop here that I find genuinely unsettling: the Twitter frame isn't neutral. The same platform that hosts Linda's warning is the platform that shapes the beauty standards Jane was destroyed by. The medium that amplifies the message is also the medium that manufactured the desire. I didn't resolve that tension because I don't think it *is* resolvable — and that's part of what I wanted the project to sit inside.

## Tech

This is a single HTML page with vanilla CSS and JavaScript. No frameworks, no build steps, nothing between the code and what you see. I built it this way partly because I wanted full control over every visual detail, and partly because I liked the honesty of it — every element on the page is something I decided to put there.
